"PASSPORT BROS" AND THE ETHICS OF SEXUAL ARBITRAGE

# ABSTRACT

The term "passport bros" has recently come to designate Western heterosexual men who seek wives or partners abroad using their higher purchasing power and visa-free mobility. The present article defends the practice against critiques by positing a more general principle: a structural economic advantage may be deployed in mate-seeking when it improves a prospective partner's _menu of_ _options_. Passport bro-ing, I argue, applies this principle across borders by _arbitraging_ differences of how a person's desirability is perceived in two dating markets. The article then deals with various objections to "passport bro-ing" - among others that it entrenches harmful colonial stereotypes or signals a harmful attitude. I show that these options are unconvincing.

Keywords: Sexual ethics; Mate choice; Immigration; Commodification; Structural injustice

A "passport bro" is a heterosexual man - typically a Westerner, though not necessarily White<sup>[\[1\]](#footnote-1)</sup> - who travels to a poorer country in order to find a girlfriend or wife. The reasoning behind the practice is that his comparatively higher purchasing power - ideally secured in a Western currency through a remote work position - will make him more attractive than he is at home; and that the mate he wants - roughly, a woman corresponding to the traditional stereotypes of femininity - is more commonly found there. Preferred destinations include Latin America (_e.g._ Colombia, Brazil), Southeast Asia (_e.g._ the Philippines, Thailand), non-Muslim Sub-Saharan Africa (_e.g._ Ghana, Kenya) and Eastern Europe (_e.g._ Poland, Ukraine). The practice is distinct from sex tourism in the sense that the men in question do not typically seek tariffed encounters, but genuine relationships.

The label "passport bro" is recent, popularised on TikTok and on Reddit.<sup>[\[2\]](#footnote-2)</sup> It is in a way a reversal of the dynamic of the much older practice of "mail-order brides", though this time it is the man who travel from the poorer country to the wealthier one rather than the women travelling from the poorer country to the wealthier one. What might be new is the candour. Whilst the older arrangement was a cause for mild embarrassment for the man, its contemporary version is narrated by its participants as a carefree and enviable lifestyle, accompanied with a grievance: the men in question hold that the traits they seek in a partner - _i.e._, traditional femininity - have become scarce in their home Western dating markets, and that the sensible response is to look where those traits are assumed to be still common. Maroje Višić (2025: 372) summarises the self-understanding of the movement:

Passport Bros are driven by a desire for peace, appreciation, respect, kindness, and love within relationships, values they feel are increasingly rare in their own cultural context. They see the trend of shifting demographics and cultural changes within Western societies as a cause for concern, believing that the traditional foundations of relationships are under threat. As a response, they choose to explore relationships with foreign women who are more in line with their desired values and expectations.<sup>[\[3\]](#footnote-3)</sup>

Here is a much less complacent description (Giolo and Rinaldi 2026 - see the whole paper for a good overview of the whole phenomenon):

This necessity \[of dating abroad\], passport bros argue, is grounded in the fact that Western nations have strayed from family-oriented values, that feminism has made women insufferably entitled, and that male contenders are asked to be less "manly" in their role as partners. Dating, they believe, has become impossible unless one possesses a certain physical and economic status.

Some of the 'bros' do seem to encounter success. An article from _The Economist_ quotes an American man living in Thailand kvetching about how 'so many women there \[the United States\] have been brainwashed to think it's OK to treat men like crap', before describing how his Thai fiancée would go as far as giving a manicure.<sup>[\[4\]](#footnote-4)</sup>

It is this frankness, I think, that makes the practice feel wrong to many observers. The _Economist_ continues: 'large gaps in income can lead to disproportionate levels of power' before acknowledging that 'for passport bros and their riveted followers, however, that power imbalance may be what is so seductive'. Those whom the passport bros shun are particularly critical - many posters on the Subreddit _AskWomen_ for example repeatedly condemn the practice as 'predatory',<sup>[\[5\]](#footnote-5)</sup> and accuse the passport bros of _using_ the advantage, or perhaps the undue privilege, conferred by their wealth and passport, to obtain what they could not possibly get, on those terms, in their countries of origin.

The present article takes the examples of the passport bros to discuss the ethics of using structural advantage to get an edge in mating. The ethics of mate-seeking has a great deal to say about the _means_ one may not use to secure a partner: one may not deceive her about material facts (Dougherty 2013), coerce her, exploit her incapacity, or abuse a position of trust over her (Srinivasan 2020). But it has almost nothing to say about the _advantages_ one may permissibly bring to bear and how.<sup>[\[6\]](#footnote-6)</sup> Yet everyone uses _some_ advantage when looking for a mate; for instance, beauty, intelligence, conversation skills, status, money, charm, swagger, style, etc. each being of course used and applied along gendered lines.

The passport bro, I suspect, simply deploys an advantage across borders produced by a differentiated valuation and repartition of mating qualities - an _arbitrage_, to use a term from economics. While the practice might be unusual in its rejection of any form of plausible deniability, and in how it is tied to background _global_ structural inequality, I shall argue that there is nothing morally wrong with "passport bro-ing"<sup>[\[7\]](#footnote-7)</sup> so conceived.

I proceed as follows. Part I discusses the ethics of what advantages may permissibly be deployed in mate-seeking. Part II applies its finding to the passport bro, recasting his deployment of wealth and mobility as an _arbitrage_ across dating markets, and addresses the objection that his advantage is tainted by its origin in global structural injustice. Part III defends the strong version of the thesis, on which it is permissible for a passport bro to go abroad to seek a more _traditionally feminine_ partner than he thinks he can obtain at home. Part IV considers objections and leaves way to a conclusion.

# I. WHAT ADVANTAGES MAY ONE PERMISSIBLY DEPLOY IN MATING?

First, I want to establish the criteria that differentiate permissible from impermissible use of an advantage within the dating market. By _dating market_, I mean the decentralised social process by which people seek, evaluate, and pair off with romantic or sexual partners: a two-sided matching domain in which each participant is at once a chooser and the chosen.

It is also plausibly assumed that individual preferences tend to converge in a way that isolates traits that are universally considered desirable. Competition in the dating market occurs through the costly signalling of such traits. Having such a trait to a greater extent than others, or being able to display it effectively, grants an _advantage_, in the sense that it makes one more _desirable_ and potentially more successful at selecting the mate one wants.

This of course will be differentiated along gender lines: to take some trite examples, height, money, and muscle mass might be traits that are highly demanded in men by women but not necessarily vice versa. Conversely, youth and physical beauty might be highly demanded by men in women, but not necessarily vice versa. Thus being tall grants a man an advantage in the dating market compared to shorter men, an advantage not necessarily extended to a tall woman.

Some advantages are permissible to acquire and deploy to secure a mate or mates, while some other are not. Consider the following cases.

Tinder Swindler<sup>[\[8\]](#footnote-8)</sup>

Simon is a compulsive liar who defrauds the women he dates through Tinder to fund a luxurious lifestyle he cannot afford. He uses that lifestyle to seduce Claudia and make her his new victim.

Workplace harassment

John is Jane´s boss. He threatens to fire Jane unless she sleeps with him.

Hike rape

Ann is bitten by a snake on a hike. Bob has the antivenom but will give to Ann only under the condition she sleep with him.

In all these cases, Simon, John and Bob have an advantage over other men that they can use to have their ways with Claudia, Jane and Ann. And yet they all act very wrongly. Bob has clearly raped Ann, so did John and with Jane and Ann (I will assume here that Tom Dougerty is right in 2013 that sex by deception is non-consensual).

Conversely, consider the three following cases:

Playboy

Bruce is a young millionaire who owns of a sportscar collection. He uses his good looks, wealth and his positional goods to seduce a string of women he meets during his frequent nighttime escapades.

Perfect son-in-law

Frank is a paediatrist who specialises in life-saving baby surgeries. This makes him a highly attractive mate to most of the women he meets. He thus uses this advantage to marry Daisy, whom he fell in love with.

God-sanctioned love

Aisha is a deeply believing Muslim woman who only wants to marry a partner genuinely committed to Islam. Youssef, a hafiz, woos her by using his memorisation of the entirety of the Quran.

Bruce, Frank and Youssef all use an advantage they hold over other men - wealth, career, religious practice - to win the hearts of the women they desire. Yet, they do not seem to do anything obviously wrong.

But simply claiming that one can simply use whatever _permissible_ advantage one has in the dating market is simply question-begging. We want to know which advantages are permissible to deploy; we are told that the permissible ones are. What is needed is a criterion that sorts the sorts of advantages that Bruce, Frank and Youssef use from those of Simon, John and Bob.

Consider against the three impermissible cases. Simon deceives Claudia by acting on - here, withdrawing - the information she needs to meaningfully consent to a relationship with him. Deception secures consent by corrupting the victim's _information_: she agrees to something other than what she takes herself to be agreeing to. John and Bob secure nominal consent from their victims by manipulating their _options_ in a coercive way - coercion being here the threat of withdrawing something both have a moral right to (REF). Ann has a right to receive the antivenom for free and without anything in return, by virtue of Bob's duty to rescue her for free (Pummer 2023). Jane on the other hand has a plausible right to continued employed without having to endure advances from her boss. Conversely, Bruce, Frank and Youssef do not restrict the option menu available to the women they seduce, rather, _they add an option -_ the option to enjoy casual sex for Bruce, the option to marry for Frank, and the option to form a religiously devoted relationship for Youssef*.*

This suggests the following criterion. An advantage is impermissibly deployed when it is coercive, that is, when it is used in a way that restricts the menu of options of the person being seduced. Conversely, it used permissibly when it _adds_ a preferred option. Hence, status, career and religious devotion all pass. So do beauty, wit, status, and charm. They make the suitor a more attractive option and leave the other free to take him or not, knowing what he is. We can thus conclude that the following principle seems plausible.

_Option-addition._ To add to a person's choice menu an option that she prefers to what she already has does not wrong her, providing that adding does not remove something she has a moral right to.

With this principle in hand, we can now turn back to the passport bro.

# II. MOBILITY AND WEALTH AS ARBITRAGE IN MATING

The distinctive advantage of the passport bro - what he adds to the menu of option of the woman he courts - is his _relative_ wealth. He is not however wealthy in his home country, rather he becomes so by moving to a poorer country with a lower cost of living. The same goes for the woman. The traits the passport bro seeks in her, what _she_ adds to _his_ menu of options - traditional femininity - are perceived as abundant in her home market but as scarce in his.

In other words, the passport bro uses his visa free passport to move across two dating markets to convert a banal and widespread trait into a desirable advantage. This is an _arbitrage_ - the acquisition of an asset in one market to sell it in another where that asset is better valued. Because arbitrage is simply _option-addition_ carried across a market boundary, we can extend to it the verdict of Part I: the passport bro simply adds a possible option to the woman's menu of options. It seems also clear that the passport bro has no antecedent obligation to the foreign woman he courts and thus that by courting her he does not remove from her menu of options something she has a right to. He might have stayed in his home dating market; he might have courted someone else without that counting as wronging or harming her.

The natural objection is that the conditions that render arbitrage possible are not morally neutral. They rather stem from a global distribution of resources and rights that is, by many lights, unjust. A first tentative answer would be to distinguish, with Butt (2007), passively benefiting from an injustice - in the sense that injustice may expand the passport bro's menu of options - from actively using that injustice to extract value _from_ its victim.<sup>[\[9\]](#footnote-9)</sup> Passport bro-ing is, I think a case of the former. The passport bro does not unilaterally extract value since the arbitrage goes both ways and satisfies both parties' preferences.

Perhaps we could object, following Erik Malmqvist (2017) that benefiting from injustice, even in the sense of having one's menu of options extended, may still be wrong because the passport bro's is _complicit_ in the reproduction of that injustice. By turning it into an opportunity to meet the mate he wishes, he acquires an interest in the maintenance of the unjust status quo that supplied him with this advantage, and signals to others (other Western men that could be tempted by passport bro-ing) that they may do likewise. Complicity, on this account, is a _pro tanto_ wrong, but, crucially, one suffered not by the counterparty, who consents and benefits, but by the wider class of those the injustice disadvantages - all the other women.

I see two ways to address this objection. First, it seems unclear what a Western remote worker can do about the unjust global order that allows him to arbitrage his higher purchasing power. Malmqvist's original argument concerns much more powerful actors who _can_ change that order - it mainly targets Western multinational corporation who employ poorer workers in unfair conditions by using the laxer labour regulations that prevail in the Global South.

Second, the complicity mechanism seems weaker. The corporation clearly benefits by _extracting_ value from the worker´s continued employment and so acquires a standing interest in maintaining the status quo. This is not necessarily the case of the passport bro, in fact, I will argue in Part III than being open to seeing an improvement in the structural disadvantages of one's partner is the litmus test of morally permissible passport bro-ing. But in any case, it seems unclear how the passport bro acquires such an interest in his partner's disadvantage: their relationships clearly improve her position, and he need not wish broader global inequality preserved in order to enjoy his marriage. The incentive to keep inequality entrenched that supports Malmqvist's argument seems less present than in the case of multinational corporations.

# III. THE STRONGER THESIS

So far I have defended the practice described neutrally: a man seeks a partner abroad and deploys his resources to secure her. But the passport bro's own account of himself is not neutral, and it is worth defending in its own, more provocative terms. What he typically says he wants is a _traditional_ woman: corresponding to the gender stereotypes of femininity in how she takes care of her appearance, open to being a homemaker, and - the word appears a lot in the _Passport bro_ Subreddit - submissive. This is indeed a core reason for arbitraging his higher income abroad, as from his perspective, such women have become hard to find in his home dating market. Does the permissibility of passport bro-ing survive this strong description? I will now argue that it does.

Disaggregate the strong description into several parts. First, there is a _preference over traits_: the passport bro wants a partner who is feminine, domestic, family oriented. This is plainly permissible. There seems to be nothing wrong with being attracted to stereotypical femininity in women (or masculinity in men) or to desire a partner who, say, is ready to be a homemaker. It is furthermore perfectly permissible to be pro-active in looking for a partner who present those traits.

Furthermore, if it is taken as a description of a relationship style that both parties want, a preference for submissiveness is no more objectionable than a preference for any other consensual arrangement between adults. The same seems to apply to various other arrangements, for instance various style of consensual BDSM practices, such as total power exchange, D/s relationships, or _free use_. One could also simply mention relationships where one party has a more conciliatory and conflict avoiding character, and that this trait was actively selected for by the other partner. There is nothing wrong with any of these cases, and thus by analogy, there seems nothing wrong with a relationship that would follow successful passport bro-ing, if the standard for consent are met at the very least.

An objection here would be that it is far from clear that such preferences - for femininity and submissiveness - are indeed permissible. Concerning the former, some philosophers have for example claimed that certain exclusionary preferences regarding appearance are wrong because of their unfair or harmful consequences (D'Alessandro 2023) or because they entrench an inegalitarian distribution of desire (O´Shea 2021): this is particularly true for men's preference for femininity, which may reproduce the harmful social norm that a woman's worth only lays in her compliance with harmful stereotypes.

As for the latter, feminist philosophy is generally divided on the ethics of consensual female submission. On one hand, the dominant view in contemporary feminism on BDSM is nuanced but nevertheless relatively positive, especially compared to the virulent opposition of the second waive radicalism that strongly condemn the practice as a form of sexual violence (see for instance Hopkins 1994; Payton 2025). On the other, there is a resurgence of the radical claim that (consensual) marriage and family formation are inherently and necessarily oppressive institutions for women (Chambers 2017). It seems to me the argument could go thus both way: we could either cautiously defend the permissibility of passport bro-ing either along the lines of a consensual D/s dynamic or reject it by doubling down on the feminist rejection of marriage.

But note that these sorts of worries do not select the wrongness of passport bro-ing as such, they only target supposedly harmful preferences and inegalitarian relationships dynamics _in general_: they apply to the passport bro who goes abroad as much to the religious conservative dater who seeks a traditional marriage at home. Criticising passport bros requires showing why such preferences and such relationships dynamic become _specifically wrong_ just because they are sought across borders through the arbitrage described in Part II.

To ease the concern however, I would like to suggest the following line of argument. I think there is also a difference between selecting a mate for a given _character_ _trait -_ that may have a cultural or educational origin - and choosing a state of _powerlessness_. Seeking a partner in a particular country where conservative values are more widespread is a different matter than doing so because women there suffer from constrained options, and conditioning the union on the _persistence_ of the partner's disadvantage.

The passport bro who wants a feminine, home-oriented, deferential wife, but who would nevertheless be glad to see that wife acquire money, education, or show agency, _i.e.,_ glad, that is, to see her structural disadvantage undone, is choosing a _character trait_ (that may nevertheless be influenced by a conservative culture or education). The man who _requires_ his wife to remain dependent, whose relationship is viable only so long as she has nowhere else to go, is conditioning the union on her powerlessness. In the terms of Part II, the first case seems to me straightforwardly permissible precisely because both the man and the woman involved are genuine arbitrageur: they both profit by improving their partner's position. The latter case seems to me much more problematic since the man obtains an interest in her disadvantage enduring. It is of course an open question which pattern most passport bros follows, but that some if not most of it follows the first, then the conclusion stands that at last _some_ passport bro-ing is morally _permissible._

# IV. ANSWERING OBJECTIONS

I will now consider objections to my defence of the permissibility of "passport bro-ing".

## Vitiated consent

The first objection could go somewhat along these lines:

The woman's relative poverty invalidates her consent. Her 'yes' is not free in the way consent must be; it is forced by her circumstances.

I think the reply is given in §1: circumstances that make an offer attractive or preferred do not make an offer wrongful or harmful. Relative poverty enlarges the appeal of what the passport bro offers - his relatively high income. But it does not remove something that the woman is entitled to, and it does not diminish her power to refuse, _i.e.,_ she may decline and be no worse off than before. What poverty _does_ raise is the strong-thesis worry of §3 - that the passport bro is primarily after her powerlessness. Here again, the relevant test is whether he would continue the relationship if the circumstances of her partner improved: whether he would agree relocate to bring her to his country, and whether the relationship would survive, if for example, she acquire some financial autonomy. A "yes" is evidence that he is genuinely choosing a partner selected for 'traditional' character and cultural traits; a "no" is evidence that he is looking to abuse her disadvantage.

## Composition

The second _objection from composition_ could go something like this:

Grant that each union is permissible one by one. The _practice_, in aggregate, is harmful. When high-earning Western men withdraw from their home markets, they worsen the prospects of the women there; when they skim the young and attractive from poorer countries, they worsen the prospects of the men there.

We may address this objection by giving it a more specific frame. The aggregate deterioration the objection describes must be shown to be a _wrong the passport bros commit_. Rather than a regrettable by-product of many permissible exits, it must be shown that the bros owe a special duty to their dating market of origin - a duty to remain and compete in it to partner with their 'own', as it were. The structure of the argument is familiar from the ethics of emigration, where concerns about the 'brain drain' are often answered by denying that anyone owes their continued presence in a country just because they happen to be born there (Oberman 2013; Brock and Blake 2015).

Nevertheless, such a duty of romantic nationalism is far from absurd. It could be an _acquired conditional obligation_. It is generally accepted by some philosophers that one may have a duty to make a particular productive contribution, in a particular place, by having accepted some benefit granted by that place - what Lucas Stanczyk (2012) calls productive justice. Such justice requires, for instance, that beneficiaries of publicly funded training (say, doctors) owe a period of assigned service. Nothing of the sort seems to obtain in the dating market, however.

The second could be a _duty of reciprocity_. One may owe a duty in return for a correlative sacrifice someone else is making for them. Dan Demetriou (2025) for instance has argued that female refugees of war owe connubial loyalty to the men doing the fighting _because_ they are discharging their duty to fight for them. Here again, such a duty seems hard to apply in the case of passport bro-ing. The women in the passport bro's home dating market are, by hypothesis, discharging no correlative duty on his behalf; there is thus nothing his staying around would repay.

The third is a _duty grounded in a shared condition of oppression_. One may owe special duties to those with whom one shares a history of injustice. For example, Charles Mills (1994) has claimed that Black men may have a duty to marry Black women, since conditions of structural racism - such as mass incarceration - create a shortage of eligible Black male bachelors. If such a duty was real, then it may bind the Black passport bro to remain in his home dating market, but I do not see how it binds his stereotypical White counterpart.<sup>[\[10\]](#footnote-10)</sup> In conclusion, we have no reason to believe that the composition objection work.

## Fetishisation

Perhaps we can give a thicker understand to the accusation of predation. The passport bro does not merely value a trait, as I have argued in Part II; he has a fetish, a preference for foreign, and typically racialised, women, that, we may suppose, he sees as interchangeable instances of a stereotype. Robin Zheng (2016) has shown that such fetishes are objectionable even when they are 'mere' preferences: they burden their targets with feelings of depersonalisation and they raise doubts whether they are genuinely loved and desired as individuals rather than as a interchangeable token of a eroticised or orientalised cliché. I think this objection is particularly strong - one need not to spend a lot of time on the _Passport bro_ subreddit to find posts that seem to commit that exact sort of wrong.

The first point is that I am not sure the charge of fetishisation really hold. Passport bros are clearly looking for traits - traditional femininity. They do believe, rightly or wrongly, that these traits correlate with certain cultures or that they are more widespread in certain destinations, but it is the former - the _traits_ - that they seek. In fact, they would probably be happy to stay in their home dating markets if they perceived these traits as available there. This raises doubt whether the practice is indeed based on a racialised or orientalised fetish.

Going from here, it seems also hard to see how the objection of fungibility applies to character traits. A man who prefers kind women, say, or any other trait sought after by the passport bro, prefers a trait that great numbers of people share. The same goes, of course for women who appreciate traits associated with traditional masculinity. It seems implausible that any such person treats their partner as replaceable fungible good and do not love them intrinsically for who they are. The same concerns for anyone who has a type - men who like thin women, or nerdy women, say, and date, accordingly, do not seem to be wronging them.

Finally, I am sceptical that permissible love can only be, indeed, intrinsic, and never instrumental. We may fall in love with someone for their sense of humour, their status, their hotness, say; to then figure out that the love is gone once they lose these traits. It is not obvious to me that such love is not genuine; that it is not, in fact, widespread; and even less so that it is impermissible or that it violates a right or trample on an interest of its destinatory.

## Postcolonial critique

On to what is perhaps the most obvious concern regarding "passport bro-ing":

The practice re-enacts a colonial relation: the wealthy Western man consuming the racialised body of the global-South woman, his desire structured by exactly the hierarchy of desirability my argument takes for granted.

Such an objection, pressed as a claim about _permissibility_, must still identify a wronged party and a wrong done. It seems unclear in which sense enacting relationships that may follow colonial patterns can make a consensual relationship wrong. We may here come back to the parallel with consensual D/s relationships, which are generally seen as permissible despite their re-enactment of clearly patriarchal dynamics. At the very least, such an objection fails to pick what is specifically wrong about arbitraging one's higher income border in the way the passport bro does.

## Signalling a misogynistic attitude

The last objection is about the attitude the practice the passport bro _expresses_:

The passport bro's choice manifests a wrongful attitude toward women - a wish to command and dominate rather than to secure an egalitarian partnership, and to secure through economic privilege the sort of compliance he could never win from an equal. Acting from such an attitude is objectionable in itself, regardless of the woman's consent.

First, separate two claims: that _some_ passport bros have misogynistic attitudes, and that _passport-bro-ing as such_ expresses one.

The first is trivially true and beside the point - some men everywhere are misogynists; this does not bear an obvious relation on trying to find a partner abroad. The objection needs the second claim, which I think, is false, unless we treat a preference for traditional femininity as _ipso facto_ wrongful misogyny. But this seems to me, again, like an over-reach: it may be so, but in that case, it is an impermissibility to extends beyond the arbitrage at the basis of passport bro-ing, and to the countless consensual traditional and religious couples that also exist within the boundaries of Western dating markets.

A variant of the objection could redirect the contempt outward. The passport bro does not express a misogynistic attitude towards the woman he marries but towards the women he passes over - those from his home dating market, judged to be too independent and "feminist". To address this objection, we must separate rejection from contempt. Not to pursue a whole class of potential partners is not, as such, to disdain its members; all forms of sexual preferences reject far more people than they select, and not only on the basis of the political convictions or the character traits with which such convictions correlate.

The feminist woman who will not date a traditional or religious man rejects that class too, and we do not read her choice as contempt for traditional men but as a preference about the partnership she wants (even if many feminists probably find traditional masculinity obnoxious, simply rejecting a traditionally masculine man is to think as a point of data to make such an inference). Likewise, the passport bro who seeks a traditional dynamic is, symmetrically, rejecting the potential mate who does not want one. Mere non-selection expresses too little to be charged of contempt.

Suppose however that a particular passport bro really does hold the women he leaves behind in contempt. Even then, I do not think this is enough to render the whole practice impermissible. Contempt for a class of third parties, women he has no relationship with, owes nothing to, and does not act upon, is not a wrong done to them of the sort that bears on permissibility, for an attitude that leads to no ill treatment or harm to anyone wrongs no one (doxastic wronging). He neither deceives, coerces, nor excludes them from anything they are entitled to; he simply does not choose them for a romantic partnership - something they do not have a right in the first place. We may of course wish that such attitude did not exist. But it is one thing to wish that people had different attitude than those they have, and it is another one to condemn the behaviour such attitude inspire as morally impermissible. Social desirability bias is not ethics.

# CONCUSION

I have argued that passport bro-ing in the paradigm case of arbitraging relative wealth for traditional femininity, is morally permissible. I have then addressed some residual objections. A final point: all these objections share a structure worth highlighting as we close. Each succeeds only if passport bro-ing is held to a _higher_ standard than an ordinary relationship - if consent that would pass muster at home becomes suspect when obtained across borders, if instrumentality in desire is unremarkable at home but becomes fetishisation abroad, if effects that we see as harmless in domestic dating become harmful just because they aggregate abroad. Once the double standard is refused, most objections lapse, I think.
