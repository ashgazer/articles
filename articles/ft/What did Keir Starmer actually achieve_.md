In his two years as UK prime minister, arguably Sir Keir Starmer’s biggest political decision was one he insisted that he did not have to make.

After winning power with a manifesto that promised no rises in the three biggest taxes, his government increased taxes by almost £70bn and spent most of the revenue on public services, particularly the NHS.

This decision was the foundation of many of the achievements Starmer cites as his legacy, such as a fall in hospital waiting lists and the end of the two-child benefit cap. Yet it was also the source of some of the political frustration and faltering growth that have contributed to his departure from Downing Street.

On Thursday, in one of the Starmer administration’s final acts, Downing Street announced new peers including London mayor Sir Sadiq Khan and Martin McTague, national chair of the Federation of Small Businesses. Sir Chris Wormald, whom Starmer ejected as cabinet secretary, and former senior judge Sir Brian Leveson will become cross-benchers

Elsewhere, Starmer has used his final weeks to shore up his legacy, with the so-called [Hillsborough Law](https://www.ft.com/content/3ee63673-86f1-45b4-969a-00f65bf588c3?syn-25a6b1a6=1) requiring candour from public officials and the defence investment plan, and he said in his final Prime Minister’s Questions on Wednesday: “I leave the country in better shape than I found it.” But in a range of areas where Starmer vowed improvement, the picture is mixed.

On the economy, Starmer first promised “the highest sustained growth in the G7” and then to raise living standards.

Since the election UK GDP per person has grown by 1.1 per cent, above Germany and France but behind the US and Japan, according to Ruth Curtice, head of the Resolution Foundation think-tank. Unemployment has risen from 4.1 per cent to 4.9 per cent.

Helped by new fiscal rules, chancellor Rachel Reeves used tax rises to significantly increase public investment. Capital spending is due to grow at twice the rate of day-to-day spending, predominantly on defence and net zero.

<iframe frameborder="0" title="Interactive or visual content" src="https://flo.uri.sh/visualisation/27090430/embed?auto=1"></iframe>

Some 90 per cent of extra day-to-day spending allocated by Reeves went to the NHS. The health budget will reach £232bn by 2028-29. But reforms to make best use of the money have been slow to progress.

Key goals in a 10-year plan for the NHS were “basically still at the design stage”, said Mark Dayan of the Nuffield Trust think-tank.

Although waiting lists are lower than in July 2024, they have risen in the past two months. Progress “does not look quick enough” to hit Starmer’s target of treating 92 per cent of routine patients within 18 weeks by 2029, he added.

<iframe frameborder="0" title="Interactive or visual content" src="https://flo.uri.sh/visualisation/27651708/embed?auto=1"></iframe>

In an administration characterised by lack of preparation for power, a standout exception was the clean energy plan overseen by Ed Miliband.

He rapidly expanded renewable energy schemes in pursuit of a goal of generating 95 per cent of Britain’s electricity from low-carbon sources by 2030, changing planning rules for onshore wind and other big projects and guaranteeing to pay fixed prices for electricity.

More than half of Britain’s electricity now comes from renewables after increases in generation, but few experts think the 95 per cent target will be met.

<iframe frameborder="0" title="Interactive or visual content" src="https://flo.uri.sh/visualisation/28421592/embed?auto=1"></iframe>

Politically, Miliband’s push towards net zero has hastened the fracturing of the Westminster consensus about the need to decarbonise the economy by 2050, driven by Nigel Farage’s Reform UK.

Housing was arguably the other key area where Labour was properly prepared. It quickly reintroduced logical building targets, liberalised planning rules, such as allowing development on parts of the greenbelt, and increased levels of social housebuilding.

Matthew Spry of planning consultants Lichfields said Starmer, and his housing minister Matthew Pennycook, had made “genuinely impressive strides” and laid the foundations to boost supply to 300,000 homes a year. But with just 130,000 housing starts in 2025-26, few expect Starmer’s goal of 1.5mn homes over this parliament to be met.

<iframe frameborder="0" title="Interactive or visual content" src="https://flo.uri.sh/visualisation/29686542/embed?auto=1"></iframe>

Although migration was not one of Starmer’s original missions, he was quickly forced to add a pledge to “smash the gangs” to deal with the voter priority of irregular immigration.

While ministers boast of a decline in small boat crossings, the number of arrivals has largely returned to where they were before a rise in Labour’s first year.

“There is absolutely no clear evidence that any of the things happening in the UK have made a difference” to levels of irregular migration, said Mihnea Cuibus of the Oxford university Migration Observatory, noting fluctuations had been driven largely by trends in migration elsewhere in Europe.

While Labour has more than halved the backlog of asylum claims, this has in part been replaced by a mounting appeals backlog.

<iframe frameborder="0" title="Interactive or visual content" src="https://flo.uri.sh/visualisation/27028753/embed?auto=1"></iframe>

A similar mixed picture is evident in Starmer’s other pledges. He promised to “recruit 6,500 teachers in key subjects”. Numbers are up 4,654 in chosen areas but there are 1,900 fewer teachers overall.

Police numbers are down overall, but up in the neighbourhood teams dealing with the priority of antisocial behaviour.

The number of children judged “school ready” at the age of 5 has risen to 68 per cent, but too slowly to be on track for a 75 per cent target by 2028.

Starmer promised “a decade of national renewal”, and the full impact of some flagship reforms — the workers’ rights package and the ban on social media for under-16s, for example — will become clear only well after he has left the stage.

Yet Hannah White, head of the Institute for Government think-tank, said Starmer’s “original sin” was failing to set up Whitehall to achieve his priorities.

“Like a lot of administrations, Starmer’s came in thinking they could operate the machine better than all their predecessors. But they couldn’t,” she said.

Even his critics acknowledge Starmer’s serious demeanour was suited to foreign affairs, where he has balanced the need to get on with Donald Trump with a refusal to become embroiled in the Iran war.

Starmer has also been widely praised for the robustness of his support for Ukraine and helping to manage fraught relations between Washington and Kyiv. But pro-EU cheerleaders inside Labour have lamented the modest terms of the reboot of UK-EU co-operation, 10 years on from Brexit.

For their part, voters themselves had one very clear ask of Starmer. Labour’s huge majority, abetted by tactical voting, was in large part a reaction against the debilitating chaos of the final years of Tory rule.

Starmer’s victory was also helped by his ruthless transformation of Labour, banishing the hard left and repairing relations with Jewish voters with a sense of purpose unmatched by anything he did in Downing Street.

For most prime ministers, walking into Number 10 marks the start of their achievements. For Starmer, in many ways it turned out to be the culmination.
