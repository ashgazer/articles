# Thinking, Fast and Slow — Everyday Matrix

Daniel Kahneman's central idea, then a practical table you can actually use.

## The one big idea

Your mind runs two systems:

- **System 1** — fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Always on. Great at routine, terrible at statistics. It generates impressions and jumps to conclusions.
- **System 2** — slow, deliberate, effortful, logical. It's lazy and would rather let System 1 handle things. You have to *consciously* switch it on.

Almost every bias in the book comes from the same root cause: **System 1 gives you a confident answer, System 2 doesn't bother to check it.** The whole practical game is learning to notice the moments that deserve a System 2 override — and not wasting effort overriding the ones that don't.

A useful phrase to remember: **WYSIATI — "What You See Is All There Is."** Your mind builds a coherent story from whatever information is in front of it and ignores what's missing. It doesn't feel like you're missing anything, which is exactly the trap.

## The everyday matrix

Each row: the bias, the everyday tell (how you catch it in the moment), and the counter-move.

| Bias | What it is | The everyday tell | Counter-move |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | First number you hear drags your estimate toward it | You're negotiating/estimating and a figure was mentioned early | Deliberately generate your *own* number before reacting to theirs |
| **Availability** | You judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind | You feel scared/confident based on a vivid recent story | Ask "is this actually common, or just memorable?" |
| **Substitution** | You answer an easier question than the one asked | "Is this a good investment?" quietly becomes "do I like this company?" | Restate the actual question out loud; check you're answering *it* |
| **Loss aversion** | Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good | You're clinging to something to avoid a loss, not to gain anything | Reframe: "if I didn't already have this, would I choose it now?" |
| **Sunk cost** | Throwing good money/time after bad because you've already invested | "I've come this far, I can't stop now" | Decide based on the future only; past spend is gone either way |
| **Framing** | The same fact feels different depending on wording | "90% success" vs "10% failure" pull you differently | Flip the frame yourself and see if your gut changes |
| **Overconfidence** | You trust your own judgment more than the evidence warrants | You feel *certain* and haven't looked for the downside | Ask "what would have to be true for me to be wrong?" |
| **Hindsight** | After the fact, you feel you "knew it all along" | "It was obvious this would happen" | Recall what you *actually* predicted beforehand, honestly |
| **Halo effect** | One good trait colours your whole judgment of someone/something | You like one thing about a person and now trust their unrelated opinions | Judge each attribute on its own evidence |
| **Confirmation** | You seek info that fits what you already believe | You're only finding evidence you're right | Actively go look for the strongest case against you |
| **Base-rate neglect** | You ignore boring statistics in favour of a vivid story | A detailed profile makes something feel likely | Ask "how common is this in general, ignoring the story?" |
| **Planning fallacy** | You underestimate how long/costly things will take | "This'll only take a weekend" | Ask how long similar things *actually* took before (yours and others') |
| **Regression to the mean** | Extreme results tend to be followed by more average ones | You credit a cause for a return-to-normal ("the criticism worked!") | Expect extremes to normalise on their own; don't over-attribute |
| **Peak-end rule** | You judge past experiences by the peak + the ending, not the average | You rate a whole trip/meal/relationship by one moment | For big decisions, reconstruct the *whole* experience, not the highlight |

## How to actually use this (the meta-skill)

You can't run System 2 on everything — it's exhausting and mostly unnecessary. The skill isn't "distrust every thought," it's **recognising the high-stakes moments where a wrong intuition is expensive**, and slowing down *only* there.

Trigger a deliberate check when:
- **The stakes are high** (money, a big commitment, a decision you can't easily reverse).
- **You feel unusually certain** — confidence is where overconfidence hides.
- **A strong emotion is driving the call** — fear, excitement, irritation. Strong feeling = System 1 in the driving seat.
- **A number, a story, or a first impression arrived early** and everything since has orbited around it.

When one of those fires, ask one question: **"What question am I actually answering, and what am I not seeing?"** That single habit — pausing to check WYSIATI — catches more of the biases above than trying to memorise all fourteen.

## Two selves (the bonus idea worth keeping)

Kahneman distinguishes the **experiencing self** (you, moment to moment) from the **remembering self** (the story you tell afterwards). They often disagree — you'll choose the *memorable* holiday over the *enjoyable* one. Worth knowing when you plan your life: are you optimising for the experience, or for the memory of it? Neither is wrong, but they're different, and most people confuse them without noticing.
