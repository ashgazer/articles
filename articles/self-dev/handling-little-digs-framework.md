# Handling Little Digs: A Framework

A practical approach for dealing with someone who often tucks small digs into otherwise normal sentences.

## 1. Name the pattern to yourself first

These comments have a shape: **a genuine point + an unnecessary extra**. The mashuni comment didn't need "I only like to eat fresh" — that's the dig riding on the back of a legitimate preference. Once you can spot the extra bit in real time, you stop reacting to the sentence as a whole and start noticing the specific clause that landed wrong. That alone takes some of the sting out — you're diagnosing, not just absorbing.

Useful internal label: **"that's the extra bit."** Not an insult, not a big drama word — just a flag you raise privately.

## 2. Decide in the moment: address, deflect, or let go

Not every one needs a response. Quick filter:

| Situation | Move |
|---|---|
| One-off, low stakes, you'll forget it by tomorrow | **Let go.** Not everything needs airtime. |
| Recurring theme (your eating, your habits, your choices) | **Address lightly**, especially if it's the 2nd/3rd time on the same topic. |
| Said in front of others / clearly meant to land | **Deflect in the moment**, revisit privately later if needed. |

You don't owe a reaction every time. Picking your moments actually makes it *more* effective when you do respond — it's not "him being sensitive," it's "he noticed this specific one."

## 3. In-the-moment responses (calibrated by intensity)

**Level 1 — Light, curious, non-confrontational** (use most often):
- "Why do you say that?"
- "What makes you say that?"
- "Is that a dig?" (said genuinely curious, not accusing — tone carries this one entirely)

These work because they hand the comment back without escalating. She either clarifies (fine, drop it) or backpedals (also fine, drop it) or gets awkward (informative — tells you it *was* intentional).

**Level 2 — Naming it plainly, no charge in your voice:**
- "That felt a bit pointed."
- "I'll take the compliment on the tuna and skip the rest."
- With the healthy-food comment specifically: "I do eat plenty of stuff, that's a bit of an assumption" — said breezy, not defensive.

**Level 3 — Direct, for repeat patterns only:**
- "I've noticed a few comments lately about [my eating / my choices] — is there something you want to actually say?"

Start at Level 1. Most of the time it's enough. You escalate only if the pattern repeats on the same topic.

## 4. What NOT to do

- Don't over-explain or justify yourself (the coke zero example — you don't need to defend your diet). Justifying accepts the premise of the dig.
- Don't go silent and stew — that's what you did with the tuna comment, and it's the option that costs *you* the most and her the least.
- Don't match energy with a bigger dig back. It muddies who's being unreasonable.

## 5. Managing your own reaction

- The sting is often less about the words and more about **not knowing if you're "allowed" to react** — that's the actual uncomfortable bit. Level 1 responses solve this because they're too mild to be seen as an overreaction, so you're never the one who "made it a thing."
- Keep a mental (or literal) tally. Two examples isn't a pattern. Five examples about the same topic is. Let the evidence build before you decide this is who she is rather than a one-off mood or awkward phrasing.
- It's fine for it to bother you even if it's "small." Small recurring things are exactly the kind of thing that wear people down precisely because each one feels too minor to raise alone.

## 6. The long game

If it turns out to be a genuine pattern (not just two isolated moments), the goal isn't to win an argument with her — it's to make yourself a slightly harder target for the *smuggled* dig, so future ones either don't come, or come out in the open where they're easier to deal with. Level 1 responses, used consistently over time, tend to do exactly that without you ever having "a big conversation."
