Shabana Mahmood, home secretary, is set to become Britain’s next chancellor, according to three people briefed on the thinking of Andy Burnham, in a prospective move that has been welcomed in the City.

[Burnha](https://www.ft.com/stream/84695f2a-7f9d-409c-b9ad-9f3865d76e67) m’s spokesperson refused to comment on “speculation” and said cabinet roles would be assigned only on Monday after he becomes prime minister.

But one person briefed on the plan said: “ [Shabana](https://www.ft.com/stream/511b2345-f47c-401b-93d9-a847c6c81169) is nailed down as chancellor. That’s definitely happening.” Markets responded positively to the prospect of a chancellor coming from the right of the Labour Party.

The first big task for the new chancellor will be to frame an autumn Budget, with Burnham saying on Wednesday that he might “ask for a little bit more” in tax.

Burnham declined to rule out imposing wealth taxes, as he warned that fiscal decisions were “going to be difficult”. He added: “You know, we are going to have to work quite hard to make sure we can pay our way. And at some point that might be having to ask for a little more.”

Asked about a wealth tax, he said: “I’m not going to rule things out right now. I do believe we need a greater sense of fairness.”

Kemi Badenoch, Conservative leader, said: “Andy Burnham isn’t even prime minister yet but he’s already talking about raising your taxes again.”

After days of speculation, people close to Burnham’s thinking say that they expect Mahmood to go to the Treasury while the original frontrunner for the role of chancellor — Ed Miliband, the current energy secretary — was now likely to become foreign secretary.

One ally of Burnham said of Mahmood’s possible appointment: “I wouldn’t steer you away from that.”

UK government bonds outperformed European peers on Wednesday as investors reacted to the news that Mahmood was seen to be leading the race for Number 11.

Mahmood is on the right of the Labour Party and is seen as a tough operator and capable minister. She has overseen contentious immigration reforms but has not previously had an economic brief in government.

Miliband, who was once a Treasury adviser, is from Labour’s soft left, and has championed net zero policies to tackle climate change. He is admired by some Treasury veterans for his economic expertise.

David Miliband, Ed’s older brother, had previously been seen by some Labour MPs as a possible foreign secretary under Burnham.

Colleagues of Mahmood confirmed that she has discussed policy issues with Burnham this month, mainly covering home affairs but also the economy.

They insist that Mahmood, who was previously justice secretary, has not been told that she is going to the Treasury and that there is a lot of “speculation” about future jobs.

Some trade unions and many Labour MPs argue Miliband’s determined pursuit of net zero is harming the economy, while corporate leaders and the City of London feared he might preside over more borrowing or pursue anti-business policies.

Burnham will be anointed Labour leader on Friday and will become prime minister on Monday, when he will formally announce his cabinet picks.

People close to the incoming premier expect him to bring back into the cabinet Angela Rayner, former deputy prime minister, and Lucy Powell, deputy Labour leader, as well as former health secretary Wes Streeting and former transport secretary Louise Haigh.

Jonathan Reynolds, chief whip, is thought likely to return to his old brief as business secretary, while Miatta Fahnbulleh, a key economic adviser to Burnham, and [Anneliese Midgley](https://www.ft.com/content/ef284dd9-60f5-4ea6-b266-574976f87b94?syn-25a6b1a6=1), another ally, are expected to get jobs.

Among those expected to leave the cabinet are business secretary Peter Kyle, technology secretary Liz Kendall, housing secretary Steve Reed and attorney-general Lord Richard Hermer.

Current chancellor Rachel Reeves and education secretary Bridget Phillipson are among those waiting to hear if they will continue in cabinet, while John Healey, former defence secretary, may get a recall.

The 10-year UK government bond yield fell 0.05 percentage points to 4.93 per cent, while equivalent yields on Italian debt rose 0.03 percentage points. Sterling rose 1.1 per cent to $1.353 by late afternoon trading in London.

“I think the market is relieved that it’s looking like the new chancellor will not be from the left of the party,” said Lee Hardman, a senior currency analyst at MUFG.

Fund managers said investors were less worried about the political risks stemming from the Labour leadership transition as they gained more clarity on the choice of chancellor.

“We need confirmation of what the policy agenda of the new administration is going to be \[but\] from our perspective we think the focus will really start to shift back to macroeconomics,” said Ranjiv Mann, senior portfolio manager at Allianz Global Investors, which has a bet for gilts to outperform US Treasuries.
